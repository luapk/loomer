# Narrative Followability — Making Stories Compelling, Creative, and Followable

Arijon's grammar gives the audience spatial and temporal coherence. This reference covers what makes that coherent sequence *worth following* — the storytelling craft that sits on top of the grammar.

A storyboard can be technically perfect (every line of interest respected, every cut motivated) and still fail. It fails when there's nothing to follow. This file is about how to make sure there is.

---

## The audience's five questions

At every cut, the audience is consciously or unconsciously asking:

- **WHO** is in this shot? Have we met them? Do we know what they want?
- **WHERE** are we? Has the geography been established? Did we travel here, or jump?
- **WHAT** just happened? What does this shot show that the previous one didn't?
- **WHEN** are we? Is time continuous from the last shot, or has it elapsed? If elapsed, is that signalled?
- **WHY** is this shot here? What does it add to the story I'm following?

A storyboard that answers all five at every cut is followable. A storyboard that withholds answers *deliberately* (knowing exactly when each will be revealed) is compelling. A storyboard that withholds answers *accidentally* is just confusing.

---

## Withholding and reveal — the engine of compelling narrative

The single biggest failure mode of literal storyboards is showing everything in order with nothing held back. A shot list that says "she walks into the kitchen, she sees the note, she reads it, she cries" is a description, not a story.

Story comes from the gap between **what the audience knows** and **what they want to know**. That gap is curated:

- **What's withheld at shot 1?** A face. A reason. The room she's walking into. The thing she's holding. The voice on the phone.
- **What's revealed at shot N?** That same withheld thing — but now we've travelled enough to feel it.
- **What's withheld until the very last shot?** Often the meaning of the whole — the line that recontextualises everything before.

**Three patterns of withholding:**

1. **Late reveal of identity** — we follow a character without seeing their face. A glove on the wheel. The back of a head. Then the reveal. Works best when the withheld face has consequence (we recognise the person, or their face tells us what their actions did not).

2. **Late reveal of context** — we see actions whose meaning is unclear. Then a wide / a cutaway / a line of dialogue locks the meaning. The retro-fit is what makes it stick.

3. **Late reveal of stakes** — we see what's happening but not why it matters. Then we discover the stakes (the deadline, the cost, the relationship) and rewatch the earlier shots in our head with new weight.

**For storyboarding:** When you do the beat-list (Step 3 of the workflow), explicitly identify the withheld thing and the reveal shot. If you can't, the storyboard probably doesn't have a story yet — it has a description. Go back.

## The information curve

Every effective narrative has an information curve — what the audience knows on the y-axis, time on the x-axis. The curve usually rises (information accumulates), but its *shape* matters.

- **Linear rise** — boring, predictable. Each shot adds one fact.
- **Slow rise then jump** — withholding for most of the piece, then a reveal that recontextualises. Most compelling stories.
- **Decoy curve** — the audience thinks they know, then a late shot reveals their understanding was wrong. Twist endings.
- **Inverted curve** — opens with full information ("she will die in the next hour"), then withholds the *how*. Tension comes from the inevitability.

**For ads:** The decoy curve is the single most common ad structure. The audience thinks they're watching one thing; the brand reveal recontextualises. Set the decoy in shot 1.

**For music videos:** The slow-rise-then-jump structure works well — withhold the connection between the song's lyric and the visual narrative until the last verse.

## Subtext through framing

The most important sentence in this reference: **what the audience *sees* and what they *hear* should never be the same thing.**

When dialogue and image agree, the scene is flat. When they create tension, the scene has subtext.

**Examples:**
- Line: "I'm fine." Shot: Her hand grips the edge of the table, knuckles white.
- Line: "We're so happy together." Shot: Two-shot from outside the window, glass between us and them, distance.
- Line: "Welcome home." Shot: Wide of an empty hallway, light flickering — "home" feels like the wrong word.
- Line: (a salesman's pitch about reliability). Shot: A close-up on his hand on the keys, slightly trembling.

**For storyboarding:** When the script gives you dialogue, ask what the *picture* is doing in counterpoint. If the picture is just illustrating the line, you're not earning the shot. The picture should be telling the part of the story the line cannot say.

In ads particularly, this is where the most awarded work lives. The voiceover says one thing; the picture says the deeper truth.

## Visual rhyme

A storyboard that rhymes is one a viewer remembers. Rhyme means a visual element — a shape, a gesture, a colour, a composition — recurs across shots in a way that creates meaning.

**Types of rhyme:**

1. **Compositional rhyme** — shot 1 frames the mother in a doorway; shot N frames the daughter in the same doorway, same composition. Generations.
2. **Gestural rhyme** — shot 1 shows him buttoning his jacket; shot N shows him unbuttoning it. Beginning and ending.
3. **Object rhyme** — the watch in shot 1 reappears in shot N, but now broken. Time has passed and cost something.
4. **Colour rhyme** — a red coat that thread through three shots, marking a character through a crowd or a journey.
5. **Lighting rhyme** — backlit silhouette in shot 1; same backlit silhouette in shot N, but the figure has changed.
6. **Match cut rhyme** — the bone in *2001* matching the spaceship; deliberately designed at the cut between two shots.

**For storyboarding:** Build at least one rhyme per piece. For a 30s ad, a single deliberate rhyme between an opening shot and a closing shot can carry the whole piece. For a music video, multiple rhymes can create a visual signature.

## The dramatic question

Even a 15-second ad has one. The dramatic question is what the audience is wondering — usually unconsciously — that pulls them through.

- **A music video for a love song**: "Will they get back together?" or "What happened between them?"
- **A car ad**: "What is she driving toward?" or "Why does she care so much about this car?"
- **A short film**: "Who is the man at the door?"
- **A pet food ad**: "Why is that dog acting strange?"

The question is set in the first shot or two. The whole piece is the audience leaning forward to find out. The final shot is the answer (or a deliberate non-answer that turns the meaning).

**For storyboarding:** Identify the dramatic question explicitly. Write it down. If the answer is given in shot 2, you don't have a question — you have an announcement. If there's no question at all, you have a description, not a story.

## The five things AI storyboards get wrong

These are the failure modes specifically of AI-generated storyboards (from observation across the field):

1. **Pretty but functionless shots.** Every shot is a beautiful image, but no shot does narrative work. The result is a slideshow, not a story. *Fix:* Every shot must answer one of: "What does the audience now know that they didn't?" or "What feeling has shifted?" If neither, kill the shot.

2. **Description not narrative.** The brief says "a woman drinks coffee, then walks to work." The storyboard gives you exactly that, in order. *Fix:* Find the missing dramatic question. What does the audience *not yet know* about her, her destination, her morning, her stakes?

3. **All shots same scale / same length.** AI tends toward the average. The average is a series of medium shots, all 5 seconds. *Fix:* Force variance — extreme close-ups, extreme wides, 2-second flashes, 8-second holds.

4. **Genericness in detail.** "A coffee shop" instead of "the brass-rail bar at a 1980s diner with vinyl swivel stools and a glass case of doughnuts under fluorescent light." The Bible solves this only if the Bible itself is specific. *Fix:* Reject generic descriptions even in the planning phase.

5. **No subtext.** The shot illustrates the line, the line illustrates the shot. *Fix:* For every shot with dialogue or VO, the picture should be doing something the line cannot do — counterpoint, irony, foreshadowing, withholding.

## Compelling, creative, followable — what each word means in practice

**Compelling**: The audience leans forward. They want to know what happens next. This is achieved through the dramatic question (set early) and withholding (sustained until the right reveal).

**Creative**: The audience encounters something they didn't expect. This is achieved through specificity (the bizarre detail in the Bible), through visual rhyme (the unexpected echo), through subtext (the picture saying what the words can't), and through structural surprise (the decoy curve, the late reveal).

**Followable**: The audience can answer the five questions at every cut. This is achieved through Arijon's grammar (the spatial coherence) and through deliberate, paced reveals of information (the temporal coherence).

A storyboard that has all three feels effortless to watch and rewards a second viewing. A storyboard with technical grammar but no compelling/creative spine is dull. A storyboard with creative ambition but broken grammar is confusing. A storyboard with both spine and grammar but no withholding is a literal description. The aim is all three at once.

## The "drop a stranger in" test

The final audit of every storyboard: imagine a viewer who has never read the brief, hasn't seen anything before this storyboard, and is dropped into any 6-shot window. Can they:

- Tell who's in the scene and how the people relate to each other?
- Tell where they are?
- Sense what's at stake even if they don't know all the details?
- Feel pulled to want to see the next shot?

If yes — even for a window in the middle of the piece — the storyboard is followable. If no, identify the broken link. It's usually one of:
- A missing establishing shot.
- A character introduced without enough specificity to read.
- A subject moved between locations without the geographic transition signalled.
- A line of dialogue that needed a reaction shot to land.

Fix the link. Re-test.
